# Dinner Questions — February 28, 2026

> *5 questions to get aligned, get fired up, and walk away from this meal with clarity. No fluff. These are designed to surface the decisions that actually move the needle.*

---

## 1. The "Opening Day" Question

**Imagine it's 90 days from now. The Shopify store is live. What are the first 20 products on the shelves?**

Talk through this specifically:
- How many are book bundles vs. individual titles?
- Are any of them digital products Scott built (discussion guides, a game, a printable)?
- Is there a "Start Here" bundle for the brand-new homeschool mom who just pulled her kid out last week?
- What's the ONE product you'd be most proud to share on social media on launch day?
- What price range are most of these products sitting in — and does that feel right for your audience?

*Why this matters: This forces you from "big vision" into "what do we actually ship first." The answer reveals your true priorities.*

---

## 2. The "Dinner Party Pitch" Question

**You're at a dinner party in Anchorage. A mom says, "I just decided to homeschool my 8-year-old and I have no idea where to start." You have 30 seconds. What do you tell her about your store — and what's the ONE thing you say that makes her pull out her phone and look it up before dessert?**

Work through it together:
- Anna, what would YOU say? Say it out loud, right now, at this table.
- Scott, what would YOU say? Say yours.
- Where did your pitches overlap? That's your core message.
- Where did they differ? That's a tension worth resolving tonight.
- Now combine them. Say it back as one sentence. Write it on a napkin. Keep that napkin.

*Why this matters: If you can't explain it simply at dinner, your website can't explain it either.*

---

## 3. The "Gift Card Fantasy" Question

**An Alaska homeschool mom just got her $2,000 state allotment loaded. She has your store open on her laptop, a cup of coffee, and two hours. Walk through her shopping trip out loud — item by item.**

Get specific:
- What does she search for first?
- What catches her eye on the homepage that she didn't expect?
- Does she find a bundle that saves her time, or does she build her own cart piece by piece?
- At what moment does she discover the "rooms in the back" — the discussion guides, the tools, Scott's AI-generated stuff?
- What pushes her cart from $800 to $1,400? What's the "oh wow, I need this too" moment?
- When she's done, does she feel like she still needs to go to Amazon or Rainbow Resource for anything? If yes — what? (That's your roadmap for what to add next.)

*Why this matters: This is your customer journey. If you can narrate it vividly, you can build it. If you get stuck, that's where your store has a hole.*

---

## 4. The "Division of Labor" Question

**It's a random Tuesday in Month 3 of running this business. What is Scott doing between 8 AM and noon? What is Anna doing?**

Be honest:
- Who's sourcing and adding new products to Shopify?
- Who's creating the digital content (guides, games, resources)?
- Who's handling customer service when someone emails with a question?
- Who's writing the social media posts? The blog? The email newsletter?
- Who's managing the money — pricing, margins, accounting?
- Is either of you doing something you'd hate? Name it now. That's the first thing you outsource or automate.
- What does Scott's teaching schedule allow? How many real hours per week does each of you have for this?

*Why this matters: Vision is fun. Execution is Tuesday morning. If you don't know who does what, you'll both do everything and burn out by Month 4.*

---

## 5. The "One Year From Tonight" Question

**It's February 28, 2027. You're sitting at this same table. The store has been live for about 9 months. What has to be true for you to look at each other and say "this is really working"?**

Put numbers and feelings on it:
- How much monthly revenue makes this feel real? ($5k? $15k? $50k?)
- How many customers or orders per month?
- Is there a community around it yet — a Facebook group, an email list, repeat buyers who know you by name?
- Has Scott built the first version of a tool or course that lives outside Shopify?
- Has anyone you've never met recommended your store to someone else unprompted?
- What's the thing that would make Anna tear up with pride?
- What's the thing that would make Scott feel like he's finally doing what he was built to do?

*Why this matters: You need a shared finish line for Year 1. Not a business plan — a gut-level picture of success that you both see the same way. If your answers diverge here, that's the most important conversation of the night.*

### SEO Strategy Notes

**Why the name matters for SEO:**
- Your domain name (e.g., homeschoolshelf.com) is a ranking signal
- Your brand name becomes the page title on every page
- When people search your brand, Google connects it to your keyword cluster
- "Homeschool" has ~550K monthly searches; "homeschool curriculum" has ~110K; "homeschool books" has ~33K

**The SEO sweet spot is:**
- **One high-volume keyword** (homeschool, curriculum, books) + **one brand word** (bright, spark, shelf, cart)
- Short enough to be a domain name
- Googleable without confusion (no video game names, no competing brands)
- Sounds like a store, not a blog

**Red flags to avoid:**
- Names with no searchable keywords (Kindlewood, Wren & Cobble — beautiful but invisible)
- Names that match existing brands (Hearthstone = Blizzard game, Basecamp = project tool)
- Names so generic Google can't distinguish you (The Learning Store, Education Hub)
