## 1. The Customer — Who Are We Really Serving?

**Maren says:** *"The homeschool market has at least a dozen distinct sub-groups. You can't serve them all equally on day one. Pick your primary audience and let the others find you organically."*

### Questions

1. When you picture your ideal customer placing her first order, who is she? (Age, location, family size, homeschool style, faith background, budget)
2. Is she a brand-new homeschooler or a veteran? Why does that matter for what we stock and how we talk?
3. What does her Wednesday afternoon look like? Where is she struggling?
4. Does she already have a go-to store? What does she wish that store did differently?
5. Is there a *secondary* audience we want to welcome but not design primarily for? (e.g., co-op leaders, afterschoolers, teachers looking for supplemental materials)

## 2. The Problem — What Pain Are We Solving?

**Maren says:** *"Nobody needs another store. They need a reason to switch. What's broken in their current buying experience?"*

### Questions

1. What are the top 3 frustrations homeschool parents have when shopping for curriculum and resources right now?
2. Is the pain more about *discovery* ("I can't find what I need"), *trust* ("I don't know if this is good"), or *overwhelm* ("There's too much and I can't choose")?
3. How do parents currently research before buying? (Facebook groups, blogs, YouTube, conventions, word of mouth?)
4. Is price a primary pain point, or are parents willing to pay more for curation and confidence?
5. What's the emotional weight of this purchase? (Curriculum buying can feel high-stakes — "Am I ruining my kid's education?")

## 3. The Offering — What Exactly Are We Selling?

**Maren says:** *"Curriculum, courses, books, toys, and games is a wide product mix. Let's figure out what leads and what supports."*

### Questions

1. Which product category do we launch with? (Full curriculum packages? Supplemental resources? Books? Hands-on learning?)
2. Are we selling *our own* products, curating *other brands'* products, or both?
3. Will courses be self-paced, live, or both? Created by us or by partner educators?
4. How does the faith-based section work architecturally? (Separate storefront? Filter/tag? Dedicated landing page?)
5. Will we carry *only* products we've personally vetted, or will we allow a broader marketplace model?
6. Do we plan to offer digital downloads, physical products, or both?
7. Any product categories that are a hard "no"? (Helps define the boundary.)

## 4. The Differentiator — Why Us?

**Maren says:** *"If you can't finish the sentence 'We're the only homeschool store that ___________,' you don't have a positioning statement yet."*

### Questions

1. Complete this: "We're the only homeschool store that ___________."
2. What would make a parent choose us over Christianbook, Rainbow Resource, Bookshark, Amazon, or a local co-op?
3. Is our edge *curation* (we pick the best), *community* (we connect families), *content* (we teach parents how to teach), or *convenience* (one-stop shop)?
4. How important is it that we serve both faith-based and secular families? Is that itself a differentiator, or just a feature?
5. Do we have any unfair advantages? (Industry relationships, a built-in audience, personal expertise, a unique content strategy?)

## 5. The Faith-Based Section — How Do We Handle This Well?

**Maren says:** *"This is the single most important brand architecture decision you'll make. Get it wrong and you alienate both sides. Get it right and it becomes a genuine strength."*

### Questions

1. What faith traditions do we want to serve? (Broadly Christian? Multi-faith? "Faith-friendly" as a tone?)
2. Should the entire site feel faith-welcoming with a dedicated faith section, or should it feel neutral with faith content clearly labeled?
3. How do we describe works that integrate a biblical worldview vs. works that are simply faith-neutral? Do we use tags, icons, filters?
4. Are there faith-based publishers or brands we already love and want to carry?
5. Would a faith-based family feel at home on the site? Would a secular family? Can both say yes?

## 6. The Brand Voice & Name — What Do We Sound Like?

**Maren says:** *"The name is the last thing we should decide, but the feel of the name is something we can talk about now."*

### Questions

1. Pick 3-5 adjectives that describe how the brand should *feel*. (e.g., warm, smart, playful, trustworthy, modern, approachable, expert)
2. Should the name explicitly say "homeschool" or be more abstract/evocative?
3. Any words or themes that resonate? (Home, gather, explore, path, roots, spark, village, lighthouse, hearth, grove, etc.)
4. Any words or themes that are off-limits or feel wrong?
5. Should the name appeal to the *parent* (the buyer) or the *child* (the learner) — or both?
6. Do we want a name that could expand beyond homeschool if the business grows?

## 7. The Business Model — How Do We Make Money?

**Maren says:** *"Revenue model shapes everything — what you stock, how you price, how you market. Let's at least sketch it."*

### Questions

1. Primary revenue: product sales (margin on goods), course fees, subscriptions/memberships, affiliate commissions, or a mix?
2. Do we want a membership or loyalty program? What would it include?
3. What's our target price point sweet spot? (Budget-conscious families? Premium curated boxes? Mid-range?)
4. Are we dropshipping, holding inventory, or hybrid?
5. What does Year 1 look like financially? What's a realistic and exciting revenue goal?

## 8. The Growth Plan — How Do People Find Us?

**Maren says:** *"In homeschool, trust transfers. If a mom in a Facebook group says 'I love this store,' that's worth more than any ad."*

### Questions

1. Where does our ideal customer already hang out online? (Facebook groups, Instagram, YouTube, Pinterest, podcasts, blogs?)
2. Would we create content (blog, YouTube, podcast) as a marketing channel? What kind?
3. Are homeschool conventions and co-op partnerships part of the plan?
4. Do we have or plan to build an email list? What would we send?
5. What's our launch strategy? (Soft launch to a small group? Big splash? Pre-launch waitlist?)

## 9. Quick-Fire Round — Gut Checks

Answer fast. Don't overthink.

1. If we could only sell ONE type of product, what would it be?
2. Name one competitor we admire. Why?
3. Name one competitor we want to be the opposite of. Why?
4. What's the ONE thing that would make a customer say "I'm never shopping anywhere else"?
5. If this business is wildly successful in 3 years, what does that look like?
6. What's the scariest part of starting this?
7. What's the part that excites us most?
